01 / Name treatment

One primary mark, two companions

All three share the same DNA: geometric sans, warm ink, and the amber signal. Option C is the selected primary; A and B are companions for formal documents and tiny surfaces.

Human and product-like at once. Light "adaptive" meets bold "os," and the amber dot is the feedback signal that keeps the system alive. Used in nav, footer, and all primary surfaces.

03 / Typography

General Sans + IBM Plex Mono

General Sans is geometric and modern without feeling cold. Plex Mono is the system's voice: labels, data, and anything the OS "says" about itself.

Rules of use

  • Headlines mix weight 700 with 300 in one line to pair confidence with calm.
  • Tracking tightens as size grows: -3% display, -2% headings, 0 body.
  • Mono is always uppercase, tracked at +14%, and never exceeds 12px equivalent.
  • No more than two weights per composition.
04 / Usage

How to keep it sharp

✓Do

  • Lead with warm paper backgrounds; reserve full ink sections for one key moment per page.
  • Use amber for interactive states, signal markers, and the feedback loop only.
  • Show the five layer colors together, as a system, when explaining the architecture.
  • Keep generous whitespace; the calm is the brand.

×Don't

  • Don't use pure white #FFFFFF or pure black #000000; everything stays warm.
  • Don't use a single layer color decoratively outside the architecture story.
  • Don't add gradients, drop shadows on type, or outline styles to the wordmark.
  • Don't let amber exceed roughly 5% of any composition.
05 / Voice

How Adaptive OS sounds

Plainspoken

Short sentences. Concrete verbs. We say "your team learns," not "organizational knowledge capture is operationalized."

Warm, not soft

We name the problem directly: running in place, broken systems, tool sprawl. Then we offer the structure to fix it.

Typographic discipline

No em dashes anywhere. Use a period, a comma, or a colon instead. Sentence case for headlines, uppercase only in mono labels.
